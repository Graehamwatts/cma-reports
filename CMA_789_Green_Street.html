789 Green Street is a 3-bedroom, 2-bath prefabricated home on a permanent foundation, built in 2001. At 1,670 square feet on a 0.179-acre flag lot, it sits in the Greenwave Community PUD in East Palo Alto with a modest $100 monthly HOA fee. The property has a detached 2-car garage and an office/bedroom area with a kitchenette.

The home is in dated condition and needs updating throughout. Paint is tired, flooring is outdated, the kitchen shows its age, the master bath has poor flow and finishes, and front landscaping requires attention. However, the 2001 construction date and solid foundation offer a newer-build advantage over many East Palo Alto homes that were built in the 1940s and 1950s.

The key constraint is the flag lot status. 789 Green Street is located at the rear of a 5-home PUD, accessible via a shared driveway easement (Common Area). Buyers must walk past four other homes to reach this property. The right side of the lot is largely consumed by the driveway, leaving limited usable yard. This configuration limits the buyer pool compared to traditional street-facing single-family homes.

The Market Story

East Palo Alto's real estate market is active and competitive right now. Over the past six months (October 2025 through April 2026), 19 comparable single-family homes in the 1,200 to 2,200 square foot range have sold, revealing strong demand and tight inventory. The median sale price is $1,099,000, with an average of $1,175,690. Homes are moving quickly when priced right, with a median days on market of 28 days and average of 42. The strongest performers are homes priced slightly below market value, which generate multiple offers and sell in under two weeks.

The comps range from $776,130 to $1,480,000, a wide spread driven primarily by condition, lot size, and home size. Price per square foot ranges from $543 to $980, with a median around $750. The newest construction homes (built around 2000-2006) command significant premiums, averaging $1,421,667 across three sales. Older homes in original condition (built 1945-1956) average $1,071,508. This tells us buyers are paying a substantial premium for homes that don't require immediate updates.

Where does 789 Green Street fit? It's a 2001-build with newer construction appeal, but in dated condition. Without renovation, it sits in an awkward middle ground: too old-feeling to compete with truly updated homes, but too new to benefit from the vintage charm or lot premium that some older East Palo Alto homes enjoy. This is where the renovation question becomes critical. A $74,000 renovation (paint, new floors, new kitchen, new master bath, front landscaping) would position this home competitively with the higher-selling newer builds, despite the flag lot constraint.

The flag lot is a real consideration. Many East Palo Alto buyers prioritize traditional single-family homes with street presence and private driveways. The requirement to walk past four other homes and navigate an easement-based shared driveway appeals to a narrower buyer pool. However, for the right buyer (investors, owner-occupants who value location and price over privacy, or those attracted to the PUD community), this isn't a dealbreaker. The key is pricing appropriately to account for this constraint.

A note on the University Square comps: 928 Wilks St ($1.48M) and 939 Oakes St ($1.475M) represent the aspirational top of the EPA market. Both are standard street-lot single-family homes with stronger market positioning than 789 Green. They're excellent reference points for upside value after renovation, but they're not directly comparable because of their flag-lot constraint and significantly larger square footage. The stronger comps for pricing 789 Green are homes in the 1,400-1,500 sqft range (801 Weeks, 2296 Pulgas, 159 Jasmine), which anchor pricing in the $1.05M-$1.35M range. Once renovated, 789 Green will likely price in the $1.20M-$1.25M range, which is the right tier for a 1,670-sqft flag-lot property in updated condition.

Our recommendation: full renovation before listing. Two scenarios have been bid: a full renovation at $74,000 (paint, flooring, kitchen, both bathrooms, office conversion, exterior/deck/landscaping) and a partial renovation at approximately $50,000 that removes the kitchen remodel and flooring. The full renovation yields approximately $150,000 in additional sale value, netting $76,000 in profit. The partial yields approximately $75,000, netting only $25,000. The extra $24,000 for kitchen and flooring generates $51,000 in additional net profit. Both options beat selling as-is, but the full renovation is the clear winner. Updated homes in this market sell in under two weeks, while partially updated or dated homes languish 20 to 90 days on market. Speed and certainty matter.

THE EXTRA $24,000 QUESTION

The real decision isn't $50K vs $74K. It's: does spending $24,000 more on kitchen and flooring net you $26,000 more in profit?

The answer is yes, and here's why. The partial renovation leaves the two most visible buyer touchpoints untouched: the kitchen and the floors. Fresh paint and updated bathrooms are great, but when a buyer walks through and sees mismatched, uneven flooring in every room and an original "funky" kitchen, they mentally subtract renovation cost from their offer. Worse, the contrast between new bathrooms and old floors/kitchen actually highlights what's dated — it creates a "half-done" impression that makes buyers nervous.

With the full renovation, the home presents as completely move-in ready. Buyers don't have to imagine anything — they just bid. That's the difference between 8-13 days on market with multiple offers (full renovation) vs 20-40+ days with negotiated offers (partial renovation).

Bottom line: Spending $24K more nets you $26K more in profit ($51K vs $25K). That's a 108% return on the incremental $24K investment. The kitchen and floors aren't optional upgrades — they're where the real money is.

Special Considerations & Key Constraints

Flag Lot & PUD Access
789 Green Street is located at the rear of a 5-home PUD, accessible only via a shared driveway easement (Common Area). Buyers must walk past four other homes to reach this property. The right side of the lot is consumed by the driveway, limiting usable yard space. Per the CC&Rs Section 2.2, all lot owners have easement rights for ingress/egress over Common Area. This significantly narrows the buyer pool compared to street-facing single-family homes. Many East Palo Alto buyers prioritize traditional homes with private driveways and street presence. However, the right buyer (investor, compact-living preference, price-sensitive owner-occupant) may view this as a feature rather than a bug, especially at a competitive price point. Expect 5-10% discount versus comparable non-flag properties.
HOA & Nonprofit Ownership
The property is in the Greenwave Community, a small PUD with five homes. The four other homes are owned by East Palo Alto Greenwave Community Association, a nonprofit mutual benefit corporation. The HOA fee is modest at $100/month, but some buyers may have concerns about nonprofit ownership and what that means for their experience, governance, or future property appreciation. Additionally, a nonprofit HOA raises questions about decision-making authority, special assessments, and long-term community stability. Disclose this proactively to buyers and be prepared to explain the benefits of a nonprofit-managed, stable, fee-controlled community.
Prefabricated Construction
789 Green Street is a prefabricated home on a permanent foundation, built in 2001. While prefab homes are legal, mortgageable, and becoming more common, some buyers and lenders have reservations. Conventional lenders typically have stricter requirements for prefab properties, including proof of permanent foundation, local building permits, and proper title status. FHA and VA loans may have additional hurdles. Be proactive: Have documentation ready (foundation certification, building permits, clear title) and work with a lender experienced in prefab homes. This is not a dealbreaker, but it requires transparency and preparation.
Office/Bedroom with Kitchenette
The property includes an office/bedroom area with a kitchenette. Depending on square footage, floor plan, and local regulations, this may or may not count as an additional bedroom for appraisal or resale purposes. If marketed as a 4-bed, an appraiser may not count it, creating a disconnect between marketing and value. Consider removing or minimizing the kitchenette to create a clean, attractive guest bedroom/office instead of risking appraiser pushback. This upgrade falls within the $74K renovation budget and removes a potential complication.
Market Timing & Interest Rates
The EPA market is active right now with strong sales velocity and prices holding firm. Interest rates and broader economic conditions are important context, but inventory remains tight and well-priced homes are moving quickly. Timing favors a quick listing after renovation. Delay risks market shifts, inventory increases, or seasonal slowdown. Act soon to capitalize on current conditions.
